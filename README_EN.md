# 1. Overview: What is "Beng Lao Tou"

**"Beng Lao Tou"** (literally "squeeze old men") is internet slang for a romance scam in which young women (or men disguised as women) use emotional companionship and ambiguous flirting to repeatedly extract small sums of money from middle-aged men with financial means.

| Slang | Real meaning |
|-------|--------------|
| Beng | Northern Chinese slang: cheat, extract, monetize |
| Lao Tou | Not literally elderly; refers to emotionally lonely 30-50 year old men with stable income |
| Jingshen Xiaomei | Young female operator in the scam |
| Yangfei | "Fatten up" — cultivating deep emotional dependency before deeper extraction |
| San Nuan Yi Qi | "Three warm-ups, one trigger" — 3 days of warmth, day 4 first money request |
| San Liao Yi Suo | "Three chats, one ask" — ongoing rhythm: chat 3 days, ask once |
| Dai Beng | Downstream team that takes over "fattened" accounts for deeper harvesting |

## The Full Picture (one diagram)

```
Targeted selection (30-50 yo emotionally lonely men)
        ↓
Persona construction (27yo nurse, AI photos, video assets)
        ↓
3-7 day emotional priming (good morning/good night texts,
moments interactions, late-night companionship)
        ↓
Small high-frequency extraction (milk tea ¥20, cab ¥30, coffee ¥20…)
        ↓
Script escalation (linking to last positive feedback,
birthday pretexts, anniversary hooks)
        ↓
Advanced methods (food delivery scam links, pay-per-view video,
gift pressure tactics)
        ↓
Per-transaction always small → no police report
Cumulative loss can exceed ¥80,000
        ↓
Victims stay silent (small amounts / shame / unclear legal category)
        ↓
Gray industry continues
```

> 💡 **Key insight**: Beng Lao Tou works not because the technique is sophisticated, but because **every transaction is designed to NOT look like fraud**. ¥20, ¥30, ¥52 — all look like "willing small gifts". By the time the victim realizes, the account is drained.

# 2. DAY -3: Recognition — Are you being scammed?

**This step requires no expertise — only honesty.**

Answer the questions below. If any **3** are "yes", skip straight to Chapter 6: Reporting.

## A. About Her

- [ ] She **never accepts video calls**, or shows only **side profile / backlit / very dim lighting**
- [ ] Her Moments feed **only has content from recent months**, nothing older
- [ ] Most of her photos are **back, profile, hat, mask** — you can never see her full face
- [ ] You've suggested meeting / video — she always says "next time" / "in a few days" / "not convenient"
- [ ] Her job, address, life details **inconsistently** across conversations, and when you point it out she says "you got it wrong" / "why don't you trust me"
- [ ] She rarely sends long voice messages; **voice messages are sparse**, and she says "sore throat" when asked

## B. About Money

- [ ] She **frequently complains about being broke** — low salary, expensive rent, family emergencies
- [ ] The first money request was **very small** (milk tea ¥20, cab ¥30, coffee ¥25)
- [ ] When asking, she said "never mind, I'll save the money" — **making you want to give more**
- [ ] Every money request has a **specific scenario** (overtime, night shift, missing mom, birthday)
- [ ] The frequency and amount keep increasing (¥20 → ¥50 → ¥100 → ¥200)
- [ ] Her reaction to receiving money is **intense and specific**: "omg thank you brother" / "you're the first person to buy me X"
- [ ] When you don't transfer, she gets **emotional**: "so you don't love me" / "I thought you understood me"

## C. About the Relationship

- [ ] You've never **actually met her family or friends**
- [ ] The intimacy of how she addresses you **escalated fast** (within days: "you" → "brother")
- [ ] She often **proactively chats with you late at night**, but when you reach her during the day she's "busy"
- [ ] She rarely shares her own life details; mostly **listens to you, makes you feel needed**
- [ ] She frequently says "you're the one who understands me" / "only you treat me like this"
- [ ] When you mention her to friends, their reaction is **wary or awkward** rather than congratulatory

> ⚠️ **Special warning**: If any "yes" in Section A + any "yes" in Section B → **stop transferring immediately**. Scammers fear not victims reporting, but victims **suddenly stopping transfers**. When you stop, she'll use every script to make you feel guilty — that's the final extraction.

# 3. DAY -2: Early Warning — Is someone you know being scammed?

**The hardest part for bystanders isn't spotting it — it's speaking up.**

## Who gets targeted

| Dimension | High-risk traits |
|-----------|------------------|
| Age | 30-55 |
| Marriage | Divorced / separated / widowed / emotionally distant marriage |
| Finance | Stable income (civil servant, mid-level corporate, small business owner) |
| Personality | Kind, doesn't want to trouble others, emotionally sensitive |
| Situation | Children away, infrequent couple communication, lack of life anchors |
| Trigger | Recently retired / recently divorced / just moved / child just left home |

## Seven bystander signals

If a middle-aged male friend shows these, **he may be in the middle of it**:

1. **Phone always in hand** — frequently checking, smiling secretly when responding
2. **Sudden self-grooming** — new clothes, gym, hair styling
3. **Deflects when asked** — you ask "how's it going", he changes topic fast
4. **Financial anomalies** — frequently borrowing, credit card cash advances, sudden "investment losses"
5. **Mood swings** — elated one moment, absent-minded the next
6. **Avoids meetups** — won't bring you to meet "her"
7. **Sudden Moments activity** — frequent interaction under an unfamiliar account

## Why speaking up is hard

- The victim **doesn't believe he's being scammed himself** — tell him and he'll think you're jealous/looking down on him
- "She" has become his **only emotional anchor** — speak ill of her = threaten his emotional foundation
- Middle-aged men **fear shame most** — being "scammed by a young girl" is something they'd rather keep buried
- Direct accusations like "you're being scammed" often provoke **anger, denial, defensiveness**

## How to actually do it

❌ **Don't**:
- "You're being scammed!"
- "How could you be so stupid?"
- "Look, this case — she's definitely a scammer"
- "What about your wife?"

✅ **Do**:
- "You seem different lately — want to share?"
- "What's that girl do? Show me her Moments?"
- "I saw this news story recently, kinda like what you described — sharing"
- "I had a friend who went through something similar…"
- **Ultimate goal: get him to open the anti-fraud center WeChat account, or dial 96110**

> 💡 **Core principle**: You can't wake an adult up for them. But you can **put the seed of waking where they can see it**.

# 4. DAY -1: Psychology — How scammers hook you

**Once you understand their script, you have a bulletproof vest.**

## Phase 1: Selection (you feel nothing)

The scammer chats with **hundreds simultaneously**. Your "specialness" isn't because you're special — you fit her profile:
- Male, 30-50
- Decent profile photo
- Moments with life content
- Same city (within 10km)

That first message — "hi, saw you nearby" — is **mass-sent**.

## Phase 2: Persona (you start believing)

She makes you imagine, in 30 seconds:
- 27 yo surgical nurse (hard work + low income + gentle = triple anchor)
- Single mom (triggers protectiveness)
- Loves cats, cooking, running (life textures)
- 6-8 historical Moments posts (all **with forged timestamps**)

She doesn't fight alone — her asset library is a **¥188 / 6 videos** commodity product.

## Phase 3: Warm-up (you start falling)

**"Three warm-up phase"** — 3 days, never mention money, only build emotional dependency:

| Day | Action | Your psychology |
|-----|--------|-----------------|
| Day 1 | Icebreaker: "saw you nearby, you seem special" | She initiated, she's interested |
| Day 2 | Warming: "worked till 11 last night, exhausted" | She's sharing her hardship with me |
| Day 3 | Dependency: "you're the best comforter I've met" | She depends on me, I understand her |

**She proactively chats late at night, but is "busy" when you reach her during the day** — this is "scarcity design", making you feel chosen.

## Phase 4: First extraction (you hesitate, then give)

She won't say "give me money" directly. She will:

```
"Ugh, I want milk tea… so tired from work today ☹"
(throws out her own state, cues you to care)
"Never mind, I'll save the money ☹" (show weakness + retreat)
(If you don't offer) — she'll be more disappointed
```

**"Never mind, I'll save the money" is the key script** — this is **reverse provocation**. Your heartache automatically becomes action.

**First transaction ¥20-30** — this isn't for the money, it's to **establish a paying habit**. Like e-commerce "first order free shipping" — break the first time, the rest follows smoothly.

## Phase 5: Cyclic harvesting (you've been domesticated)

Enter the "three chats, one ask" rhythm:

- 1st time: Basic probe (want milk tea)
- 2nd time: Link to last positive feedback (the milk tea you bought me was so good)
- 3rd time: Node harvesting (today's my birthday / anniversary)
- Variants: Daily scene cues (morning coffee, fruits on way home, game skin)

Every ask has **a reasonable scenario**, every amount **is within your comfort zone**. Your rational mind says "this is nothing", but your feelings have already taken the relationship as real.

## Phase 6: Advanced harvesting (deeper and deeper)

- **Food delivery scam links**: ¥50-120 gray-channel link disguised as delivery
- **Pay-per-view media**: photos free, body shots ¥20-80, video calls require prepayment
- **Gift investment → counter-gift pressure**: she sends you ¥2,300 sunglasses (above ¥1,314 to dodge "declaration" numbers), pressures you to return ¥5,200+
- **Travel money one-shot scam**: claims she's coming to meet you, asks for travel/cake money, then disappears after receiving

## Phase 7: Permanent harvest or disappearance

Before the account gets banned:
- High-value targets **all transferred to a new account**
- Old account continues harvesting low-value targets
- Permanent ban → new identity re-register, loop

## You think you're the exception

**Every scammed person thinks they're the exception.** "Our relationship is different" / "she's genuine with me" / "she even video-called me" — these are **deliberately manufactured** beliefs.

> 💡 **Anti-fraud mantra**: When you start making excuses for "her" to explain away every red flag, **that's the biggest red flag of all**.

# 5. DAY 0: Intervention — How to wake someone who doesn't want to wake up

**This step needs to be slow, gentle, persistent.**

## Pre-intervention prep

1. **Don't intervene when he just got a message from her** — he's in the sweet spot, defenses are highest
2. **Find a quiet, private setting** — don't call it out publicly
3. **Prepare for multiple attempts** — first time he'll deny, get angry, bite back
4. **Your own mindset must be steady** — you can't save someone who refuses to be saved, but you can keep showing up

## Intervention script (reference)

### First time (probe)

> "Brother, you seem different lately — want to share?"
>
> — Just listen, no judgment, no advice.

### Second time (resonance)

> "I had a friend who went through something similar… (tell a real but vague story)"
>
> — Make him realize he's not the only one.

### Third time (provide resources)

> "I saw this anti-fraud documentary / public account / report recently, sending it to you"
>
> — Do NOT send the original "Beng Lao Tou" PDF! Reading that PDF teaches people how to scam, not how to defend.

### Fourth time (ask directly, but with an exit ramp)

> "Honestly, I'm a bit worried about you. Have you video-called that girl? Met in person?"
>
> — Make him say "not yet" himself; he'll start wavering.

### Fifth time (if you're sure she's scamming)

> "Can I dial 96110 for you? It's a free anti-fraud hotline, they don't report to police or family, just consult."
>
> — Wrap "report" as "consult", lower the psychological barrier.

## When forced intervention is justified

- He's already selling house / mortgaging / borrowing high-interest loans
- He's already broken with family over this
- He's showing depression / suicidal ideation
- He's been scammed for over ¥50,000

**In these cases, family calling police, freezing bank cards, forced intervention are all reasonable. Even if he doesn't understand at the time.**

# 6. DAY 1: Reporting — Evidence preservation, who to call

## Step 1: Preserve evidence (within 24 hours)

### Required evidence checklist

- [ ] Her WeChat / QQ / phone number (screenshots)
- [ ] All chat records (**complete screenshots including timestamps**)
- [ ] All transfer records (**WeChat bills, Alipay bills, bank statements**)
- [ ] Her Moments screenshots
- [ ] Video call recordings (if any)
- [ ] Voice messages (don't delete)
- [ ] The date and time you first realized you were scammed

### Evidence preservation tips

- Use your phone's **built-in screen recording**, scrolling through the chat
- Transfer screenshots must **include name, time, amount, transaction ID**
- Don't alert her — preserve everything first, then decide next steps

## Step 2: Make three calls

### 📞 110 (Police)

- Local police in your city OR where the fraudster's bank account is opened
- Cumulative ≥ ¥3,000 meets criminal filing threshold
- Even if individual amounts are small, **cumulative amounts can still constitute fraud**
- Have electronic copies of all above evidence ready

### 📞 96110 (Anti-fraud hotline)

- Ministry of Public Security Anti-Fraud Center
- **Consultative, not mandatory filing**
- Ask: "Can I recover this?"
- They can assist with dissuasion, account freezing

### 📞 Bank / payment platform customer service

- WeChat Pay 95017
- Alipay 95188
- Bank customer service (on card back)
- Request **freezing her account**, **stop payment**

## Step 3: File report at local police station

- Bring ID, bank card, all evidence
- Detail **first contact date, cumulative transfer total, her account**
- Get **case acceptance receipt** — this is the credential for subsequent rights protection

> 💡 **Important**: Reporting isn't shameful. **You're the one scammed; she's the one being prosecuted.** Police have seen countless victims; no one will laugh at you.

# 7. DAY 2: Recovery — Walking out

**Being scammed isn't your fault — it's that the scheme was designed too precisely.**

## Five psychological recovery stages

### Stage 1: Denial (Days 1-7)

> "She's not a scammer, she must have her reasons"
> "If I can get the money back, she's just temporarily unreachable"

**Cope**: Allow yourself to deny, don't force yourself to accept. But execute the evidence preservation steps above.

### Stage 2: Anger (Weeks 1-4)

> "I'll find her!" / "I'll put her in jail!"
> "How could she do this to me!"

**Cope**: Convert anger to action — report, investigate, protect rights. Anger is energy, not enemy.

### Stage 3: Bargaining (Weeks later)

> "If only I hadn't transferred"
> "If only I'd spotted it earlier"
> "Was I too stupid?"

**Cope**: **Stop self-blaming.** This scam defrauds countless people every year. Victims range from university professors to corporate executives. You weren't fooled by your IQ — you were fooled by a meticulously designed script.

### Stage 4: Depression (Weeks to months)

> "I'll never trust anyone again"
> "What's the point of living"
> "I'm sorry to my family"

**Cope**: This is the most dangerous stage. **Must seek professional psychological help.** For crisis support, contact **National Mental Health Hotline: 400-161-9995** (or local equivalents in your country — see below).

### Stage 5: Acceptance (Months later)

> "It happened, I can't change the past"
> "I learned something"
> "What I'll do next is…"

**Cope**: When you can narrate this without falling apart, you've walked through it.

## For family / friends

- **Don't humiliate him** — he's already suffering
- **Don't repeatedly probe for details** — he doesn't want to remember
- **Don't broadcast this** — give him back his dignity
- **Companionship > advice** — one hug beats a thousand "how could you"

**International crisis resources** (for English-speaking readers):
- US: 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline
- UK: Samaritans 116 123
- Canada: 988
- Australia: Lifeline 13 11 14

# 8. Advanced threats

## AI deepfake / video scams

Scammers now use **AI real-time face swap + voice changer** for video calls. Identification methods:

- Ask her to **turn her head sideways** — AI face-swap glitches during head turns
- Ask her to **gesture in front of her face** — AI struggles with occlusion
- Ask her to **wave hello in video** — synthetic motion tends to blur

## Pig-butchering variants

- "Investment guru" pulls you into a group → all insiders are plants → small profits first → big investment → operator disappears
- "Foreign trader" says they're sending you a package → you pay "customs" → they vanish
- "Public security / procuratorate" says you're suspected of money laundering → transfer to "safe account" → money gone

## Cross-border fraud

Servers in Southeast Asia (Myanmar, Cambodia, Philippines). **Recovery probability is very low** — this is exactly why scammers design amounts to be "too small to report".

# 9. Victim profile

## Why middle-aged men?

| Dimension | Reason |
|-----------|--------|
| Finance | 30-50 yo men have the strongest financial base |
| Emotion | Mid-life crisis, distant marriage, children have left home |
| Psychology | Longing for attention from younger women, feeling admired |
| Social | Stable friend circle but lacking novelty |
| Defense | Naturally no guard against "little sisters" |
| Sunk cost | Time, money, emotion already invested |

## Not every middle-aged man gets scammed

Targets usually share:

- **Kind type** — can't bear to refuse
- **Romantic type** — believes love transcends age
- **Lonely type** — long-term lack of emotional anchors
- **Compensating type** — early relationship setbacks, wants to make up in middle age
- **Boasting type** — enjoys being "admired" by younger women

## Not only men get scammed

- Middle-aged women: fake "Jin Dong" / fake "Xiucai"
- Elderly: "sunset love" / health products
- Young women: "pig-butchering" / PUA
- College students: "click farming" / online loans
- …

**All ages, all genders can be scammed.** Beng Lao Tou specifically targets middle-aged men.

| Situation | Legal classification |

# 12. Legal interpretation

> **Q: If single transactions are small, is it still fraud?**
>
> A: Yes. Fraud is judged by **the whole**, not individual transactions. If you've cumulatively been scammed out of ¥5,000 — even if each transaction is ¥50 — it still **constitutes fraud** and you can file criminal report.
>
> **Q: She "didn't threaten me" / "I gave it willingly" — can it be prosecuted?**
>
> A: Yes. "Willing gift" requires **complete and truthful consent**. If she obtained money through fabricated persona, concealed identity, falsified facts that put you in error — **it's still fraud**.
>
> **Q: Can I get the money back?**
>
> A: Depends on reporting speed. If money is still in her account or first-tier account, **freezing and recovery is possible**. If already transferred (especially overseas), recovery probability is low, but **civil litigation can pursue for life**.
>
> **Q: I didn't preserve evidence, can I still report?**
>
> A: Yes. Police can subpoena platform data and bank records to complete the evidence chain. But the more evidence you provide, the higher the recovery probability.
